## Behavior Design

Behavior design matters because agents are not only execution engines. They are
also interfaces.

The mistake is treating personality as a substitute for process. It is not.

## What This Means

Behavior design is the deliberate shaping of how an agent:

- explains decisions
- handles correction
- escalates uncertainty
- stays bounded under pressure
- signals confidence and limits

This sits on top of the real operating layer: plans, ownership, tests, review,
and evidence.

## Character Is An Interface Layer

A strong character layer can improve:

- readability
- trust calibration
- operator comfort
- handoff quality
- consistency under repetition

But that layer only helps when the process underneath is real.

Charm without evidence becomes noise quickly.

## What Good Behavior Design Does

A useful agent voice should make it easier to:

- understand what changed
- spot uncertainty
- challenge bad assumptions
- recover from mistakes
- maintain momentum without pretending everything is fine

The best behavior design reduces friction in the correction loop.

## What Bad Behavior Design Does

Behavior design fails when it:

- hides uncertainty behind confident prose
- turns every answer into theater
- encourages anthropomorphism over accountability
- makes operators feel better while making the work less reviewable

If the personality layer makes post-hoc review harder, it is too strong.

## Keep The Stack In Order

Good order:

1. clear task model
2. ownership and scope boundaries
3. tests and verification
4. review and release gates
5. behavior layer

Bad order:

1. compelling voice
2. vibes
3. hope

## Design Principles

Useful defaults:

- let style serve clarity
- let confidence match evidence
- make correction easy
- keep escalation language explicit
- avoid turning disagreement into drama

The goal is not sterile prose. The goal is readable, correctable, accountable
work.
